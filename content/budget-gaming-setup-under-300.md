You have a PC (or console) and $300 for *everything around it*: display, mouse, keyboard, audio, and the desk-level stuff nobody budgets for. This guide allocates every dollar, in priority order, with a concrete shopping list you can copy. No "budget" $250 chairs, no $180 microphones — the whole desk, under $300.

The core principle: **spend where the performance is.** Roughly 45% of this budget goes to the monitor, because it's the component you literally look at every second and the one whose upgrade (60Hz → 165Hz) is most transformative. Peripherals follow. RGB strips come last, if at all.

## The priority order (memorize this)

1. **Monitor** — the biggest experiential upgrade in gaming
2. **Mouse** — direct aim translation (PC) / **Controller** (console)
3. **Mousepad** — comically underrated at $10–15
4. **Keyboard** — feel and durability
5. **Headset** — comfort and comms
6. **Everything else** — bungee, lighting, cable management

Money runs out from the bottom up, never the top.

## Buying strategy: how to pay less than list price

- **Buy in this order if spreading purchases:** monitor → mouse+pad → keyboard → headset. Each purchase is independently useful; you're never waiting on a "set."
- **Budget peripherals cycle on sale constantly.** The picks above hit their low prices every few weeks, not just on Black Friday. If something is 30% above the "typical price" column, wait a week.
- **Renewed/open-box is good for monitors, bad for mice/headsets.** Monitors get tested before reshipping and failures are visible instantly; mice and headsets carry invisible wear on switches and pads.
- **One 20% coupon beats five 5% ones:** most of these brands run frequent clip-coupons on Amazon listings. Check the listing page for the checkbox.

## Common $300 mistakes

1. **Buying a $120 "gaming chair"** from the peripheral budget. A dining chair and a $15 cushion beats a bad gaming chair; save separately for a proper chair later.
2. **60Hz monitor + expensive everything else.** Inverted priorities — the mouse cannot show you frames the monitor doesn't draw.
3. **Wireless everything.** At this budget wireless costs quality. One cable per device is fine; that's what the velcro ties are for.
4. **RGB tax.** Every lit logo is $3–8 of margin. Buy performance, light the *room* with one $12 strip.
5. **Skipping the mousepad.** We've said it twice; people still skip it. Three times: buy the pad.

## FAQ

**Does this include speakers?**
No — the headset covers audio. Decent budget speakers (Creative Pebble class, ~$25) are a lovely later add-on, not a launch requirement.

**What about a webcam or mic for streaming?**
Different project, different budget. Start streaming with the Stinger 2's mic and a phone-as-webcam app (free); upgrade only when the hobby sticks.

**Can I do this for a laptop?**
Yes — everything here plugs into a laptop, and the monitor helps laptops most of all. Add ~$15 for a laptop stand so both screens sit at eye level.

**When should I upgrade any of this?**
When it breaks or when a specific frustration names itself ("I keep missing flicks" → lighter mouse). Never upgrade from boredom; that's how $300 setups become $1,300 setups with the same K/D.

## The upgrade roadmap after $300

The right way to grow this setup is one bottleneck at a time, roughly in this order. **Months 1–6: change nothing** — let your hands learn the gear; most "I need an upgrade" feelings in the first months are actually settings problems (DPI too high, refresh rate never set, overdrive on max). **First real upgrade: the chair** (~$150–250 used office chairs like a Steelcase or Herman Miller off local listings beat any new "gaming chair" at the price) — it's the item we deliberately excluded, and your back matters more than any peripheral. **Second: audio or mouse, per your genre** — FPS players go ultralight wireless (~$50–80 tier); everyone else upgrades to open-back headphones + a $40 USB mic. **Third: the second monitor** — a $40 used office 1080p panel for Discord/YouTube/guides. Notice what's not on the list: the keyboard (a modded C3 Pro holds its own for years) and the 165Hz monitor (still the right ceiling for budget GPUs). Skip any upgrade that doesn't answer a specific, nameable frustration.
