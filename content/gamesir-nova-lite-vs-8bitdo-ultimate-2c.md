There is a new floor in the controller market, and it is stupidly good. For under $30 you can now buy a wireless pad with Hall effect sticks that will almost certainly never drift — the exact feature Sony and Microsoft still charge $70 to *not* include. Two controllers own this ultra-budget tier: the **GameSir Nova Lite** and the **8BitDo Ultimate 2C**. Both are excellent. They are also aimed at slightly different people, and picking wrong means paying for connectivity you'll never use or missing a mode you actually needed.

## Connectivity: the real dividing line

This is where the two genuinely differ, so start here.

**GameSir Nova Lite** is **tri-mode**: a 2.4GHz USB dongle, Bluetooth, and USB-C wired. That Bluetooth is the headline feature at this price — it means the Nova Lite pairs with a phone, tablet, Switch, and Steam Deck without hunting for the dongle. If you play cloud games (Xbox Cloud, GeForce NOW) on a phone, or bounce between a PC and a Switch, the Nova Lite is built for you.

**8BitDo Ultimate 2C** is, on the base model, **2.4GHz dongle + USB-C wired only** — no Bluetooth. 8BitDo's reasoning is defensible: Bluetooth adds latency and they'd rather not ship a mediocre version of it. The dongle mode is genuinely low-latency, close to wired. But if you wanted to throw this pad at your iPad, you can't. (Note: 8BitDo's naming is a minefield — there's an "Ultimate 2," an "Ultimate 2 Wireless," and Bluetooth-only Switch variants. Confirm the exact listing says **2C** and lists the dongle.)

**Verdict:** Nova Lite wins on flexibility; the 2C wins on the *quality* of the one wireless mode it ships. If you only ever plug a dongle into a PC, that difference evaporates.

## Sticks and triggers: both dodge the drift bullet

The whole reason these controllers exist:

**Nova Lite** uses **Hall effect analog sticks** — magnetic, contactless, effectively immune to the wear-based drift that kills DualSense and Joy-Con. Its triggers, however, are typically **conventional (non-Hall) analog triggers** on the Lite model, which is fine but not the linear-Hall dream. It also includes a **6-axis gyroscope** for motion aiming, which is a real perk for Switch shooters and gyro-enabled PC games.

**Ultimate 2C** also uses **Hall effect sticks**, and on current revisions extends Hall effect to the **triggers** as well — smoother analog braking in racing games and no trigger scratchiness over time. No gyro on the 2C, though, which matters if you're a Splatoon/gyro-aim person.

For the drift question that made you read this article, they tie: buy either and stick drift is essentially off your worry list for years. The tiebreakers are gyro (Nova Lite) vs Hall triggers (2C).

## Latency and gaming feel

In wired and dongle modes both are fast enough that no human loses a match to them. If you measure, the **Ultimate 2C's dongle tends to edge out** as the lowest-latency of the two, and 8BitDo's face buttons and d-pad have a slightly crisper, more mechanical snap — 8BitDo's retro-fighting-game heritage shows in the d-pad especially, which is one of the best you'll find this cheap.

The **Nova Lite's** buttons are good but a touch softer/mushier by comparison, and its d-pad is serviceable rather than special. On Bluetooth the Nova Lite adds the usual 8–15ms — fine for platformers and RPGs, noticeable to twitchy players in shooters. Use its dongle when it counts.

## Build, comfort, and battery

**Ultimate 2C** feels like the more premium object: a fuller Xbox-style grip, tighter tolerances, matte shell, and a reassuring density. Battery runs a strong **~20–25 hours**, charged over USB-C. 8BitDo's **Ultimate Software** (PC and mobile) is the best in the budget space — remapping, stick/trigger sensitivity curves, dead zones, and vibration all tunable, with firmware updates that actually arrive.

**Nova Lite** is lighter and a little more hollow-feeling, with a translucent-shell option and RGB accents that look cool but scream "budget" up close. Comfortable enough for long sessions, just not as planted in the hand. Battery is a more modest **~10–15 hours** depending on RGB and rumble use, and GameSir's Nexus app covers the tuning basics (dead zones, remap, trigger travel) but is a step behind 8BitDo's polish.

## Honest downsides, spelled out

**GameSir Nova Lite:** shorter battery life than the 2C; non-Hall triggers; the build and d-pad feel a rung below 8BitDo's; RGB translucent aesthetic won't suit everyone; Nexus software is functional but rougher. Bluetooth latency means you'll still reach for the dongle in competitive play, slightly undercutting its own flexibility pitch.

**8BitDo Ultimate 2C:** no Bluetooth on the base model, so no casual phone/tablet pairing; no gyro; the model-naming chaos makes it genuinely easy to buy the wrong "Ultimate" by accident; it costs a few dollars more; and there's no back-paddle/extra-button story here — it's a clean standard pad, not a feature pad.

## FAQ

**Do both work with Steam and Steam Deck?**
Yes. Both are recognized by Steam Input as Xbox-style controllers, and both work on Steam Deck (wired or via dongle). Enable Xbox configuration support in Steam → Settings → Controller and per-game remapping lives there too.

**Will either work on Xbox or PS5 consoles?**
No — neither is licensed for Xbox or PlayStation, so they won't work as pads on those consoles. These are PC / Switch / Android / Steam Deck controllers. If you need Xbox console support, that's the licensed PowerA territory covered in our [controllers under $40 guide](best-budget-pc-controllers-under-40.html).

**Is the extra ~$8 for the 2C worth it?**
If you're PC-first, yes — the better d-pad, Hall triggers, longer battery, and superior software earn it. If you specifically need Bluetooth or gyro, no amount of build quality replaces a feature the 2C simply lacks; get the Nova Lite.

**Do Hall effect sticks really not drift?**
They read stick position with a magnet and no physical contact, so there's nothing to wear down the way potentiometer sticks do. "Never drift" is the marketing; "very unlikely to drift for years" is the honest version. Either way it's a categorical upgrade over the sticks in a stock DualSense or Joy-Con.

**Can I use these for shooters instead of mouse and keyboard?**
On PC without aim assist, mouse and keyboard still win for competitive FPS — a controller is for racing, sports, fighting, platformers, and couch play. ## Setup tips for either pad

Three minutes on day one pays off. First, **prefer the 2.4GHz dongle over Bluetooth** on the Nova Lite whenever latency matters, and plug the dongle into a front USB port (or a short extension) rather than behind a metal case — both pads are effectively wired in dongle mode when the signal path is clean. Second, **shrink your dead zones**: Hall effect sticks are precise enough to run 3–5% inner dead zones instead of console defaults, which makes small aim corrections feel instant — do this in GameSir Nexus or 8BitDo Ultimate Software. Third, **update firmware before you play**: both brands ship fixes for early-batch quirks, and 8BitDo in particular pushes meaningful updates, so connect the pad to the software once on arrival.
