Wireless gaming mice used to start at $80. Then two companies had the same clever idea: skip the rechargeable cell, run the whole thing off a single AA battery, and sell it for the price of a decent wired mouse. The result is the two most popular sub-$50 wireless gaming mice in the world - the **Logitech G305 Lightspeed** (~$40, often $30 on sale) and the **Razer Viper V3 HyperSpeed** (~$50). Both give you real, lag-free 2.4 GHz wireless with none of the "gaming wireless is laggy" nonsense from a decade ago.

They are close on price and identical in concept, but they are aimed at genuinely different hands and priorities. This is not a "which brand wins" post - it's a "which one is right for *you*" post, and the honest answer hinges on your hand size and whether you count grams.

## Pick this one in 20 seconds

- **Smaller-to-medium hands, want RGB and onboard profiles, cheapest possible wireless:** [Logitech G305 Lightspeed](aff:B07M6Y7355)
- **Medium-to-large hands, competitive FPS, want the lightest body and best switches:** [Razer Viper V3 HyperSpeed](aff:B0D1YXXTGP)
- **Both nail:** true low-latency wireless, single-AA convenience, flawless sensors, no charging cables to manage.

If you already know your grip and hand size, that's basically the whole decision. If you don't, our [gaming mouse grip-type walkthrough](how-to-choose-gaming-mouse-grip-type.html) is worth five minutes before you spend a cent - grip fit outranks every spec below.

## The one thing they share: both run on a AA battery

This is the headline feature and the reason both mice exist at this price. Neither has a built-in rechargeable pack. You drop in a single AA, and it runs for weeks. That has real consequences most reviews skip:

- **You never wait to charge.** A dead mouse is a 10-second battery swap, not a "plug in and play wired for two hours" situation.
- **You should not use alkaline batteries.** Buy one 4-pack of Eneloop (or any low-self-discharge NiMH) rechargeables and a cheap charger, roughly $18 total, and your "battery cost" for the life of the mouse is basically zero. Swap the flat one onto the charger, drop the fresh one in, done.
- **Battery weight is real.** A AA cell weighs ~23-27 g, and both listed weights *include* it. That's why neither hits the 45-50 g of a wired ultralight - a chunk of the mass is the battery you're carrying.

Here's the part competitor roundups won't give you - the actual battery math:

| Battery approach | 2-year cost (heavy use) | Hassle |
|---|---|---|
| Alkaline AA, replaced ~monthly | ~$25-35 in batteries + landfill | Buy/store spares |
| One 4-pack Eneloop + charger | ~$18 once, then $0 | 10-sec swap every few weeks |
| (For contrast) built-in rechargeable | $0 | Plug in / wait when flat |

Rated runtimes: the G305 does about **250 hours** on one AA at 1000 Hz; the Viper V3 HyperSpeed is rated to roughly **280 hours** at 1000 Hz. Both figures collapse if you push higher polling rates (more on that trap below). For 2-3 hours a night, either mouse goes 6-10 weeks per charge. Battery is a non-issue for both - just buy the Eneloops.

## Weight and shape - where they split

This is the real fork in the road.

**Logitech G305:** 99 × 68 × 38 mm, **99 g** with battery. It's a small, mildly ergonomic-symmetrical shape shared with the old G Pro Wireless lineage. Low hump, compact footprint. Small and medium hands claw or fingertip it comfortably; large palm-grippers will feel it's a bit short. At 99 g it is *not* light by 2026 standards - it feels solid and planted rather than nimble.

**Razer Viper V3 HyperSpeed:** 127.1 × 63.9 × 39.9 mm, **82 g** with battery. The clever bit: it's physically *larger* than the G305 but **17 g lighter**, thanks to a modern thin-wall shell. It's a longer, flatter true-symmetrical shape (right-handed buttons) built for the Viper esports lineage. Medium and large hands in any grip get along with it; very small hands may find it long.

So the pattern is counterintuitive: the smaller mouse is the heavier one. If you want *light*, the bigger Viper is the lighter mouse. If you want *compact*, the G305 is smaller in the hand but you carry more grams. There's no wired-ultralight here either - for sub-60 g you'd cross into wired territory like the HyperX Haste in our [wired budget mouse roundup](best-budget-gaming-mice-under-30.html).

## Sensor and switches

Both sensors are, for any human, perfect - so this comes down to switches and headroom.

- **G305:** Logitech HERO sensor, up to 12,000 DPI, **mechanical** main switches (Omron-style, rated ~10M clicks). Clicks are crisp and a little firm.
- **Viper V3 HyperSpeed:** Razer Focus X 26K sensor, **Gen-3 optical** main switches rated ~60M clicks. Optical switches can't develop the classic "double-click" fault mechanical switches sometimes do after a couple of years, and they feel lighter and snappier.

In practice you will never out-aim either sensor. The meaningful edge is the Viper's optical switches: lighter actuation for fast tapping and immunity to the double-click gremlin that eventually bites some budget mechanical mice. It's a genuine, if small, durability and feel advantage. The same "specs won't decide your duels, fit will" logic we argued in the [G203 vs DeathAdder wired shootout](logitech-g203-vs-razer-deathadder-essential.html) applies here.

## The budget-wireless pitfalls nobody warns you about

Category-specific traps that catch first-time wireless buyers:

1. **The 8K polling upsell.** The Viper V3 HyperSpeed *can* hit 8,000 Hz polling - but only with Razer's HyperPolling dongle, sold separately for ~$30. That turns a $50 mouse into an $80 one and cuts battery life hard. Stock, it's 1000 Hz, which is completely fine. The G305 caps at 1000 Hz, full stop. **For 99% of players, 1000 Hz is the right answer** - don't let the polling-rate marketing pull you upmarket.
2. **Tiny USB-A dongles get lost.** Both ship a small USB-A receiver. The G305 hides its dongle *inside the mouse* under the top shell - brilliant, use it. The Viper's lives in a slot too; don't toss the box before you find it.
3. **No USB-C, no Bluetooth.** These are pure 2.4 GHz gaming dongles. Great latency, but no "pair with my laptop over Bluetooth" fallback. If you need multi-device, look elsewhere.
4. **"Wireless = lag" is dead.** Both are indistinguishable from wired in blind testing. This myth should not factor into your decision at all.

## So who should buy which?

**Buy the G305 if** your hands are on the smaller side, you like a compact mouse you can flick with your fingers, you want a splash of RGB, or the price gap matters and it's on sale for $30. It's the safe, cheap, proven pick - millions sold, dead-reliable.

**Buy the Viper V3 HyperSpeed if** you play competitive shooters, you want the lightest body and the snappiest optical clicks, and your hand is medium or larger. The extra ~$10-15 buys you 17 fewer grams, better switches, and a more modern sensor - meaningful if aim is your priority, pointless if it isn't.

The tie-breaker for the undecided: **shape.** A slightly-too-big mouse cramps you; a slightly-too-small one just feels quick. Small hands lean G305; big hands lean Viper. And whichever you pick, put it on a surface that lets the sensor and your arm work - see our [mouse pad speed-vs-control guide](how-to-choose-gaming-mouse-pad.html), because a $10 mouse mat changes a wireless mouse more than any DPI setting.

## FAQ

**Is single-AA wireless actually good, or a cost-cutting gimmick?**
It's genuinely good. The latency is identical to premium rechargeable mice, and never charging is arguably *more* convenient than a rechargeable that dies mid-match. Just use Eneloops, not alkalines.

**Which is better for Valorant / CS2?**
Viper V3 HyperSpeed for most people - lighter, optical switches, and a shape built for aim. Small-handed players who prefer a compact mouse can absolutely win with the G305 too.

**Do I need the 8000 Hz HyperPolling dongle?**
No. It's a niche pro-tier feature that costs extra and drains the battery. Stock 1000 Hz is what nearly everyone should run.

**Are these overkill if I just want a nice everyday wireless mouse?**
A little, but they make great daily drivers - silent-ish, no charging, reliable. If you don't game, a cheaper office mouse saves money; if you do both, either is a fine one-mouse solution.

**How do these fit a whole budget build?**
Comfortably - both leave room for a monitor and keyboard. See where a mouse should sit in your total spend in the [complete under-$300 setup guide](budget-gaming-setup-under-300.html).

## Bottom line

These are the two mice that made good wireless cheap, and neither is a mistake. **Small hands and a tight budget → Logitech G305. Bigger hands, lighter is better, competitive FPS → Razer Viper V3 HyperSpeed.** Buy a 4-pack of Eneloop AAs with whichever you choose, set polling to 1000 Hz, DPI to 800, and forget about batteries and lag for years.
