Here's the only rule that matters at this budget: **buy refresh rate, not resolution.** A 24-inch 1080p panel at 144Hz+ will do more for your gaming than a 27-inch 1440p panel at 60Hz, and under $150 you cannot have both. The jump from 60Hz to 144Hz is the single most transformative upgrade in gaming — bigger than any mouse, keyboard, or headset — because it changes what you can *see*: smoother motion, less blur, and roughly 5–7ms less average input-to-photon latency.

The budget monitor market is flooded with no-name panels, and unlike mice, a bad monitor is a bad purchase for five years. Here are the four we'd actually buy, and the traps to avoid.

## 1. AOC 24G2 / 24G2SP — best overall under $150

**Typical price: $110–140**

The [AOC 24G2SP](aff:B09FRRR2GL) is the budget monitor that reviewers have recommended for half a decade, in successively better revisions. You get a 24-inch 1080p **IPS** panel at 165Hz, ~1ms MPRT response, FreeSync Premium (G-Sync compatible in practice), and — rare at this price — a fully adjustable stand with height, tilt, and swivel. IPS means colors and viewing angles that VA and TN panels at this price can't match, so it doubles as a good all-purpose screen.

**Honest downsides:** IPS glow in dark scenes is visible (an IPS trait, not a defect). Contrast (~1000:1) makes dark-room horror games look grayish next to a VA panel. The OSD joystickless button controls are annoying. HDR is nominally supported and not worth enabling.

**Buy it if:** you want the best-rounded panel and a proper adjustable stand.

## 2. KOORUI 24E4 — best price-to-refresh ratio

**Typical price: $85–110**

The [KOORUI 24E4](aff:B0B25XCZHD) is the aggressive-value pick: 24-inch 1080p **VA** panel at 165Hz, FreeSync, for regularly under $100. KOORUI is a sub-brand of HKC, one of the world's largest actual panel manufacturers, which is how the price works. VA gives you ~3000:1 contrast — dark scenes look genuinely better than on the AOC.

**Honest downsides:** VA smearing — dark pixel transitions leave faint trails in fast motion, noticeable in dark fast games (Apex at night, Hunt: Showdown). Tilt-only stand with no height adjustment (budget $20 for a VESA arm, or use books — really). Quality control is more variable than AOC; buy from a seller with easy returns and check for dead pixels on day one.

**Buy it if:** you want 165Hz for around $100 and mostly play bright, fast games.

## 3. Sceptre E248W-19203R series — best flat-out cheapest with a warranty story

**Typical price: $75–100**

Sceptre's [E24 series](aff:B07R7XZDQL) at 75Hz — or its 165Hz gaming variants when on sale near $100 — is the floor of what we'd recommend. Sceptre is a legitimate US-based budget brand with actual customer service, which cannot be said for most sub-$100 Amazon monitor listings. Panels are unremarkable but honest, and the built-in speakers (bad, but present) can be a stopgap.

**Honest downsides:** the 75Hz models are only worth it if your total budget is truly capped — 75Hz is a small upgrade over 60Hz. Stands are tilt-only and wobbly. Color accuracy out of the box is mediocre; nudge the presets.

**Buy it if:** the budget is $80, hard stop. Otherwise stretch to the KOORUI.

## 4. AOC 27G2SP (27-inch) — only if you sit far back

**Typical price: $140–160**

Same excellent formula as the 24G2SP but at 27 inches. The catch: 1080p at 27 inches is ~81 PPI, and you *will* see pixel structure at normal desk distance. At 90cm+ viewing distance (console-at-a-desk setups, deep desks) it's fine and the extra size is immersive.

**Honest downsides:** the PPI issue is real at arm's length; text looks fuzzy next to a 24-inch panel. Often creeps over $150 — only buy on sale.

**Buy it if:** your eyes sit 90cm+ from the screen. Otherwise the 24-inch is sharper and cheaper.

## Panel types in 30 seconds

- **IPS:** best colors and viewing angles, weakest contrast. Best all-rounder.
- **VA:** best contrast (deep blacks), but dark-scene smearing in fast motion. Best for singleplayer and movies.
- **TN:** fastest raw response, worst everything else. Mostly obsolete at this budget — skip unless you play CS professionally on a $90 budget.

## Setup mistakes that waste your new monitor

1. **Not setting the refresh rate.** Windows ships at 60Hz. Settings → Display → Advanced → set 144/165Hz. An absurd number of people skip this.
2. **Using the wrong cable.** Use **DisplayPort** for 165Hz where possible; the HDMI port on budget monitors is often limited to 120Hz at 1080p. A DP cable is usually included.
3. **Enabling max overdrive.** Budget panels at max overdrive produce inverse ghosting (bright halos). Use the middle setting.
4. **Enabling "HDR."** Budget HDR400-class HDR is dimmer and washed out. Leave it off.

## FAQ

**Is 1440p possible under $150?**
Occasionally on sale (usually VA panels around $140–150), but you're then feeding 78% more pixels — a budget GPU that pushes 165 fps at 1080p pushes ~100 at 1440p. At this budget, 1080p/165Hz is the coherent choice.

**Do I need a 240Hz monitor?**
The 60→144 jump is enormous; 144→240 is subtle and costs GPU headroom. Not at this budget.

**FreeSync or G-Sync?**
Every monitor above is FreeSync and works with NVIDIA's "G-Sync Compatible" mode over DisplayPort. Enable it in the driver panel; it eliminates tearing below your refresh cap.

**My games only run at 70 fps — is 144Hz pointless?**
No. You still get lower input lag and tear-free variable refresh, plus desktop smoothness. And your next GPU inherits the headroom.

## Pairing the monitor with your GPU (quick sanity check)

A 165Hz panel is only as good as the frames feeding it, so match expectations to your hardware. Roughly: a GTX 1650/RX 6400-class card pushes 100–165 fps in esports titles (CS2, Valorant, Rocket League) at 1080p low — full benefit. An RTX 3060/RX 6600-class card holds 144+ in most multiplayer games at medium — full benefit almost everywhere. Big singleplayer games on any budget GPU will sit at 60–90 fps — where FreeSync earns its keep by keeping that range smooth and tear-free. There is no budget GPU that makes a 165Hz purchase a mistake; there are only games where you'll use half of it today and the rest after your next upgrade.

One caution in reverse: if you're on integrated graphics for now, buy the monitor anyway (desktop at 165Hz is still lovely, and esports titles run on iGPUs at low settings) — but don't also buy a 240Hz panel "for later." Buy the panel your next two years will actually use.
